Abstract / Introduction

This website serves as my official co-op work term report for the Fall 2025 term at the University of Guelph. My goal is to provide readers with a clear, honest, and engaging account of what I learned, how I grew, and what it means to work with real-world data in a professional Salesforce consulting environment.

I completed my work term at Momentum Worldwide, where I worked remotely in a hybrid-optional role as a Data Analyst. My term began on August 25th and focused on preparing, transforming, validating, and importing client data from legacy CRM systems into Salesforce. Through this experience, I gained hands-on exposure to data migration workflows, Salesforce’s data model, and the importance of clean, well-structured information in real business and nonprofit contexts.

I hope readers come away from this report with an understanding of how technical skills, communication, and quantitative reasoning intersect in industry—and how much growth can happen when theory meets practice. I also gained exposure to professional workflow tools used in many workplaces, including Jira for tracking tasks and project progress, and Clockify for work-hour reporting and accountability.

Information About the Employer

Image: The company, Momentum.

Momentum Worldwide is a Salesforce consulting firm that specializes in designing, implementing, and supporting customized Salesforce solutions for a variety of clients. Their work spans data migration, system integration, analytics, automation, and long-term platform optimization.

Operating within the Salesforce ecosystem places Momentum at the intersection of several computer science domains, including cloud computing, data engineering, AI-driven analytics, and systems integration. Their toolset includes technologies such as Salesforce Data Cloud, Einstein AI, MuleSoft, and Tableau, all of which rely on robust back-end data pipelines and sophisticated system architecture.

Based in Toronto, Ontario, Momentum is a small but highly specialized team of approximately 20 people. This size creates a collaborative environment where junior team members are exposed to real client work early on. The company’s services include custom Salesforce implementations, migration from legacy CRMs, analytics solutions, and ongoing training and support. What stood out most to me was how deeply technical accuracy and human communication were intertwined—clean data only matters if people can understand and use it. On the delivery side, tasks were often structured and tracked through Jira, and time was reported through Clockify, both of which reinforced professional project discipline.

Goals

What were your goals / learning outcomes for this work term?

My primary learning goals were:

  • Communication: Develop the ability to explain complex data findings, Salesforce data relationships, and technical processes clearly and confidently to both technical and non-technical audiences.
  • Salesforce CRM Mastery: Build a strong working knowledge of Salesforce—specifically the Account, Contact, and Gift data models—to design, validate, and implement clean data integrations.
  • Quantitative Literacy: Enhance my ability to interpret, analyze, and present quantitative information using Excel, SQL, and Python to support data-driven decision-making.
Did you develop goals relating to your job tasks?

Yes. Each of these goals directly reflected the real expectations of my role. My work required me to explain technical decisions to clients, manipulate large datasets responsibly, and understand how Salesforce structures relationships between objects. Rather than being abstract, these goals were tied to daily responsibilities. Keeping tasks updated in Jira helped ensure my day-to-day work stayed aligned with project priorities and deadlines.

What skills did you want to learn? How will these benefit your next work experience?

This work term helped me develop both technical and professional skills.

On the technical side, I strengthened my ability to clean, validate, and transform messy real-world datasets. I learned how CRM systems structure information, how migrations are planned, and how errors propagate if left unchecked.

On the professional side, I learned how to communicate clearly, ask better questions, document my work, and operate independently. I also learned how to receive feedback constructively and adapt quickly. These skills will directly benefit my next work experience by allowing me to onboard faster, communicate more confidently, and contribute meaningfully from the start. Accurate time logging through Clockify also helped me build a habit of accountability and better estimation for similar tasks in the future.

What technologies did you want to work with and why?

I was particularly interested in working with Salesforce because I wanted to understand why CRM platforms are so central to modern organizations. Cloud-based systems, customer data pipelines, and large-scale automation were all unfamiliar to me before this role.

Rather than choosing Salesforce because it is popular, I chose it because it was unknown. I wanted to understand how real companies store, move, and use data—and how mistakes in these systems can have real consequences. Momentum gave me the opportunity to see how Salesforce is actually used in practice, not just in theory. I also gained exposure to how teams operate in practice—planning and tracking work in Jira and recording effort accurately with Clockify.

Reflecting on your goals, what goals did you complete?

I successfully completed all three learning goals.

  • I became more confident in communicating technical information.
  • I developed a strong practical understanding of Salesforce’s data model.
  • I became more comfortable working with large datasets using Excel, SQL, and Python.

Some goals were achieved more deeply than others, but each one showed measurable growth. Maintaining clear progress updates in Jira and tracking hours in Clockify helped reinforce consistency, planning, and professional accountability.

What goals were you not successful in completing and why?

While I met my goals overall, I did not engage as deeply with formal Salesforce Trailhead modules as originally planned. This was primarily due to time constraints and the fact that hands-on project work became my main learning method. Instead of structured modules, I learned through direct application, documentation, and problem-solving. Although this diverged from my original plan, it ultimately resulted in deeper practical understanding.

Job Description

My role at Momentum Worldwide was Data Analyst, with a focus on data migration and quality assurance for Salesforce implementations.

I worked with client datasets extracted from legacy CRMs. These datasets were often inconsistent, incomplete, or incorrectly formatted. My job was to clean, restructure, validate, and prepare this data so it could be safely imported into Salesforce without breaking relationships between objects.

Some of the most interesting aspects of my job included:

  • Designing mappings between old and new systems
  • Creating deduplication logic
  • Detecting structural inconsistencies
  • Troubleshooting import errors
  • Writing Python scripts for automated quality checks
  • Using SQL to validate record relationships
  • Communicating findings to teammates and clients

Most of these skills were learned on the job. While my coursework gave me foundational exposure to databases and programming, this role taught me how those skills are used in real production environments. Tasks were often broken down and tracked in Jira, which helped make priorities and blockers visible, and work hours were recorded through Clockify, reinforcing real-world consulting habits.

Conclusions

If someone were asked to describe this website, I would want them to say that it demonstrates growth—technically, professionally, and personally.

This work term taught me that data is not just numbers in a spreadsheet. It represents people, transactions, histories, and relationships. Mistakes matter. Clarity matters. Communication matters.

Momentum helped me understand what it means to be responsible for data that real organizations depend on. I learned to slow down, verify assumptions, and think critically about every transformation I made.

More than anything, this experience gave me confidence that I can contribute meaningfully in technical environments—and that learning does not stop when the code runs successfully. I also gained practical experience with how teams stay organized and accountable in professional settings, using tools like Jira for coordination and Clockify for accurate reporting.
